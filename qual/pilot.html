Introduction

Welcome! In this project you will read simplified Chinese sentences with Chinese idioms and their English translation. Your task is to evaluate and categorize the translation of Chinese idiom.

This qualification HIT will train you to perform this task. You must be able to:

  • Understand Chinese idioms (or look up their meaning)
  • Evaluate the quality of translated Chinese idioms
  • Identify translation type in each instance

Translation Categories

Please read the annotation categories below carefully since they are different from what you have seen previously.

Good Translation - idiom translation is of good quality. It further divides into the following subcategories:

  • Correct translation of underlying meaning: English phrases are translated correctly and reflect the underlying meaning of the Chinese idiom.
  • Literal translation and coherent with context: English phrases are translated (near) literally from Chinese idoms, and the literal translation is coherent with the context. This subcategory is used when idiom's underlying meaning is different from literal meaning.

Mistranslation - idiom translation is incorrect due to wrong choices of words or phrases, and it affects our understanding of the translated sentence.

Unnatural - the translation is ok but not perfect enough due to improper choices of words or grammar errors. In other words, the translation can be improved by more appropriate choices of words.

Literal Translation - idiom is translated literally and translation is not coherent with context. In other words, the correct translation is supposed to be the underlying meaning, which is not the same as the literal meaning.

Addition - In addition to the translation of idiom, the translation also contains non-present content in the source. Addition can be viewed as correct translation + additional irrelevant content.

Partial Translation - idiom is translated partially. Idiom is associated with a central meaning (core info) and additional information as a supplement (modifier). For instance, an idiom may be used to describe someone is in great sadness. "Sadness" will be the core info of the idiom, and "great" will be additional information describing "sadness". We require idiom translation to have both accurate core info and modifier. Note: the modifier may or may not appear in Chinese idioms explicitly. Based on the this, we have the following subcategories:

  • Missing Modifier: Modifiers are not present in the translation.
  • Missing Core Info: Core phrases of idioms are not translated.
  • Inaccurate Modifier: Modifier is translated but not completely accurate.

Repetition - translation of idioms contain repeated words or phrases, or their synonyms. In this category, we require the repeated part itself to be correct translation, otherwise it is considered to be Mistranslation.

No Translation - there is no translation of the idiom in the English sentences. It could be due to the following reasons:

  • Chinese copy-pasted or paraphrased: Idiom is present in Chinese in English sentences, either copy-pasted or paraphrased.
  • Missing from the output: Idiom cannot be found in English sentences.
For No Translation, only the idiom in the Chinese sentences is highlighted.

More than one error category - Presence of more than one error types. You need to write down what error types there are!

Other - Other translation errors that cannot be described by categories above. You need to be specific about what the types are - don't be vague!

Binary Questions

You will also be asked two additional questions with yes or no as an answer.

  • Is the idiom used correctly in Chinese text? You may find the original Chinese sentence doesn't use the idiom very properly. In this case, you should select "No" for this question.
  • Is the boundary of idiom clearly identified in the translation? You may also find that the English translation is not able to tell the span of idiom apart from the context. The translation considers part of the idiom together with its surrounding context. For instance, we have "_ABCD_", where "_" is the context and "ABCD" is the idiom. Instead of translatig "ABCD", the English part is translating "_A", "BC" and "D_" separately.

Severity Scores

Same as before, in addition to the error cateogries, you will also be asked to choose one severity score for idiom translation. Selection of severity scores should be based on how idiom translation impacts our understanding of the whole sentence. We have three levels of severity scores: 1 - minor, 2 - somewhat, and 3 - a lot.

Confidence Scores

You will be asked to put down your confidence on your annotation. We have three levels of confidence scores: 1 - minor, 2 - somewhat, and 3 - a lot.

Key things to rememeber:

  • Select the category for idiom translation.
  • Always annotate the category you've selected
  • Add the severity and confidence score to the annotation.
  • For categories where text inputs are required, make sure the text is concise.

Note: if the given idiom occurs multiple times in the Chinese sentence, then you should annotate all occurrences and corresponding translations.

Otherwise, you should try your best to select exactly one category for the given idiom and its translation.
